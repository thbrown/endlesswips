Babies need a lot of stuff. Okay, they don't need a lot of stuff, but they do need some things to keep them safe, warm, fed, and healthy. And some products make your life as parents easier, which can be totally worthwhile.

BabyCenter provides a fairly solid calculator to help you estimate the costs of the baby's first year of life. It helps break down common baby purchases based on survey of BabyCenter parents and their research.

There are items BabyCenter lists that we'll cover in other articles that I exclude from the calculator:

  • Childcare (to be written)
  • Diapering - including diaper setup costs (diaper pails, changing pads, etc.)
  • Feeding (to be written) - cost of food/formula
  • Savings for College (to be written)
  • Medical Costs (to be written)

Removing all of those... your baby's first year may cost: $6,292!?!? That's a pretty big number. What does this include, and how much did we actually spend on this stuff?

So we spent $1,597 on our baby's first year of stuff (most of which we will be able to use for baby #2). We did borrow and were given some items, which we could have purchased ourselves for ~$239 (adding this to what we actually spent = $1,836), so one could reasonably expect to spend ~$2,000. We were fortunate enough to have a family who gave us their hand-me-downs (which were mostly used items for them as well). We had more than enough clothing from these friends, and our family and friends couldn't resist buying additional adorable clothes. Everything is so cute when it's so small!

Not everyone is as lucky as we were, but even if you do need to buy clothing, buying used is way cheaper than new, and it's often in pretty good shape because little babies grow so fast and, well, mostly just lay there. So the Clothing category is a good place to cut costs.

Free baby swap groups

I joined a "free baby swap" group on Facebook. Here's the group description:

"This is a FREE swap for baby, kid, and other gear/items in the <city> area - no buying, selling, or trading allowed within the group."

This is a great idea - people post things their kid doesn't need anymore (grew out of, never liked, etc.), and people who need it arrange for pickup and it gets a new, free life. We've gotten a few fun toys this way, and gifted some of the hand-me-downs that didn't work for us.

People are very generous in these groups, posting large boxes of clothing that would set you up with everything your baby needs for various ages. There is a bit of work involved to look for items you need and pick them up. Because of groups like this, even if we hadn't had a hand-me-down source of clothing, I believe we could have spent very little on clothing in our baby's first year.

Baby safety kits - not worth it

An obvious (to me, now) area where I don't think it's worth spending the $$ is "baby safety kits", with 20-30 pieces of "health items". This example one is $30. To quote Lucie's List, "Youâ€™ll never use all that crap. I promise."

Here's a rough breakdown of what's included:

Useful

  • Soothing scrub cradle cap brush

    • Hospital gave us one of these, but an old toothbrush also works. Could also buy separately for $5.

  • Steady grip nail clippers

    • You'll need some, they're ~$4

You'll want a higher quality product

  • Newborn nasal aspirator

    • NoseFrida is way better; we never used the nasal aspirator they gave us at the hospital

  • Digital 3-in-1 thermometer with 5 probe covers

    • You want to be confident you have a good, fast thermometer - this one might be fine but we preferred a brand name thermometer

  • Toddler toothbrush with easy-to-hold handle

    • You won't need a toothbrush until your baby has teeth (maybe even after the first year - wiping them with a wet washcloth was fairly effective). We like this banana one as a teether, mostly.

Not very useful/already have

  • Bottle medicine dispenser

    • We used a tiny syringe they gave us at the hospital. Our infant acetaminophen also came with its own dispensing syringe.

  • 6 antimicrobial alcohol wipes

    • You probably already have these in a first aid kit. Our doctor also gave us ~20 at our first visit.

  • Easy grip brush and comb

    • Baby likely won't have enough hair to actually need a brush/comb, and when they do you can just use a comb/brush you have, or the cradle cap brush.

  • 5 emery boards, emery board case

    • We didn't really use our emery boards because baby's nails were too soft

Useless (use a piece of paper or your smartphone)

  • Feeding tracker bracelet
  • Emergency information card
  • Dosage tracking card

So, for $30, you can buy a much higher quality set of items that you'll actually use: NoseFrida ($15), high-quality thermometer ($10), and nail clippers ($4).
